Before the Show

A short prep list. Knock these out the week before and install day goes smoothly.

Hanging hardware

Pieces should be wired for hanging. That’s our preferred setup — it’s the fastest to install and the most stable on our wall panels.

That said, we can work with almost any standard hanging method. Common options include:

Picture wirePreferred
Braided wire across two D-rings or eye-screws on the back of the piece. Fast, stable, what we recommend.
Sawtooth hanger
Small metal bracket with a notched edge. Works fine, especially on lighter pieces.
D-rings (no wire)
Two D-rings, one screw per ring. Solid for medium-weight work.
Eye-screws / eye-hooks
Drilled directly into the frame or stretcher bar. Wired version is preferred.

If you’re not sure what your piece has on the back, look before install day. Adding hardware in the parking lot is stressful.

Title cards

Each piece needs a title card. Bring them with you on install day — we don’t print these for you.

What to include:

  • Artist name
  • Social handle(s)
  • Title of the piece
  • Medium
  • Year
  • Price
  • A payment QR code (Venmo, Cash App, etc.) so buyers can pay you instantly

Size: roughly 3×5 inches. Bigger than that eats into wall space and your neighbor’s real estate.

Design is up to you. Some artists print on cardstock, others use stickers. As long as the info is readable from a step away, you’re good.

Pricing your work

Most first-time artists underprice. Quick check before you commit:

  • Add up materials + your time at a rate you’d be okay walking away with.
  • Add a small markup — you’re also paying for the wall, the crowd, and the rent on your studio.
  • Round to memorable numbers ($150, $275, $400) so people can think about it quickly.
  • Bring a small range of price points if you can — an entry-level item moves faster than a single $1,200 piece.

Prints & small merch

Sell prints, stickers, pins, zines, and other small items from a bin, box, or short table on the floor under your displayed work. No extra charge for this — it’s part of your hanging space. Just keep your setup tidy and don’t encroach on your neighbor.

What to bring

  • Your art
  • Title cards (one per piece)
  • Payment setup (Square reader, Venmo / Cash App / Zelle QR codes, plus some small bills for change)
  • Business cards or postcards with your work and handle
  • Your own screw gun (optional but recommended — see Installation Day)

Installation Day

Install window for your show: check your acceptance email.

If you have 5+ pieces or anything close to the 36×36 max, arrive in the first hour. Wall locations are first-come, first-served, and the prime spots fill quickly.

Salon-style hanging

Your work hangs salon-style on 8×4 ft wall panels — floor to ceiling, edge to edge. Depending on how many artists show up at once, you may share a panel with someone else. Be ready to negotiate placement.

What we provide

  • Walls (the panels we build for the show)
  • Screws
  • Screw guns (shared — you may wait for one during peak install)
  • Lighting

What to bring on install day

  • Your own screw gun if you’re on a tight timeline — ours get backed up around peak hours.
  • A level if you’re particular about straight lines.
Hammers and nails are not permitted. Screws only.

During the Show

Show hours: check your acceptance email.

  • You keep 100% of sales. We don’t take a commission and we don’t process payments for you.
  • We recommend staying near your work — it’s the easiest way to introduce yourself to people, answer questions, and collect contacts for future commissions. Not required, but it pays off.
  • Free entry for the exhibiting artist. Sorry, no plus-ones or assistants on the guest list.

Live Painters & Body Painters

If you’re exhibiting on the wall, skip this section.

Live painters

Arrival window: check your acceptance email.

Set up after the event starts — please don’t arrive earlier, we may need the floor for production setup. Bring drop cloths to protect the venue’s floor.

Body painters

Arrival window: check your acceptance email.

Same rule on setup timing — after the event starts. Bring drop cloths.

Models: body paint models get in free, but maximum 2 per artist. Anyone else with you pays cover.

Tear-Down

All art must come down and go home with you immediately after the show ends. The venue may close earlier than midnight or run later depending on the crowd — we’ll let you know if that’s happening.

Important: We don’t have access to the venue once we leave. If you forget your work, we can’t retrieve it from the venue on your behalf. Neither Pancakes & Booze nor the venue is responsible for left-behind or damaged artwork.

Promotion Toolkit

The show gets bigger when artists help spread the word. Five things you can do:

  1. Follow us on Instagram and Facebook. Our paid ads target our followers and their friends, so this directly amplifies your reach.
  2. Grab the flyer from our tickets page — save the square version to your phone and repost it now and then.
  3. Tag us in posts and stories. Use @pancakesandbooze and hashtags #pancakesandbooze + #pancakesandbooze[yourcity]. We repost a lot of tagged content.
  4. Like and share the Eventbrite event — once you pay your hanging fee, hit Like and share the listing to your socials.
  5. Pitch local media. Local blogs, TV, radio, and newspapers love a "local artist in upcoming show" angle. Email us for a press release template (coming soon — we’ll send one over manually for now).

After the Show

The night doesn’t end when the lights come up. A few small habits turn a good show into long-term momentum:

  • Send thank-you DMs to anyone who bought a piece. A short message goes a long way.
  • Restock your online store with anything that didn’t sell. The momentum from a live show carries online for about a week.
  • Post a recap — behind-the-scenes shots, the wall before vs. after, your favorite conversation. Tag us so we can share it.
  • Save buyer contacts for next time — they’re likely to come back for your next show or commission.

Photo & Social Tips

Your content during install and at the show is honestly some of the best material we get. A few things that work well:

  • "Get ready with me" install content — loading in, hanging your work, setting up your bin. Time-lapses do really well.
  • Shoot before the room goes dark. Around the start of the event the lighting is best for clean shots of your wall.
  • Use the show as backdrop. The marquee, the gallery walls, and the griddle line are recognizable and on-brand.
  • Tag everything. @pancakesandbooze + #pancakesandbooze + your city hashtag. We share the best stuff.

Sales Tax

Sales tax handling is your responsibility — rules vary by state, city, and even by event type. Some places require a temporary seller’s permit for one-day events; some don’t. Most casual artist-level transactions fall well below thresholds that anyone enforces, but we’re not your accountant. Check your state’s rules if you’re unsure.
